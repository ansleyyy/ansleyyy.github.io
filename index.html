Eating disorders

"Eating disorders are serious mental illnesses, NOT a lifestyle choice."











Types of Eating Disorders



Anorexia Nervosa

People generally view themselves as overweight, even if they're dangerously underweight. They tend to constantly monitor their weight, avoid eating certain types of foods, and severely restrict their calorie intake.

Bulimia Nervosa

Patients of Bulimia Nervosa experience a viscious cycle of binging and purging. During a binge, the person usually feels thay they cannot stop eating or control how much they are eating. Then they attempt to purge through methods like force vomitting, fasting and excessive exercise to compensate for the calories consumed and relieve gut discomfort.

Binge Eating

They typically eat unusually large amounts of food in relatively short periods of time and feel a lack of control during binges. Additionally, they do not restrict calories or use purging behaviors to compensate for their binges.


Pica

Individuals would crave non-food substances such as ice, dirt, soil, chalk, soap, paper, hair, cloth, wool, pebbles, laundry detergent, or cornstarch.

Rumination Disorder

People regurgitate the food they have previously chewed and swallowed, re-chew it, and then either re-swallow it or spit it out.

Avoidant Food Intake Disorder

Individuals experience disturbed eating due to either a lack of interest in eating due to a lack of interest in eating or distaste for certain smells, tastes, colors, textures or temperatures.


Causes of Eating Disorders



Culture and Environment

Unhealthy perceptions about body image, as promoted by the media as well as peer and parental pressure.Past traumatic experiences such as physical, emotional or sexual abuse.

Other Psychiatric Disorders

Eating disorders often co-occur with other psychiatric disorders most commonly mood and anxiety disorders, obsessive compulsive disorder and alcohol and drug abuse problems.

Genetics

Evidence suggests that genes and heritability play a part in why some people are at higher risk for an eating disorder, but these disorders can also afflict those with no family history of the condition.
